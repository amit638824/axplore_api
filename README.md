# 🟢 UNIT–I: Intellectual Property Rights (IPR)

## 🔹 Introduction to IPR

**Intellectual Property Rights (IPR)** are legal rights that protect creations of the human mind. These creations include inventions, literary works, artistic works, designs, symbols, names, and images used in commerce.

### Why IPR is Important:
- Encourages innovation and creativity
- Provides legal protection to creators
- Prevents unauthorized use or copying
- Gives economic benefits to creators
- Promotes fair competition

---

## 🔹 Types of Intellectual Property Rights

### 1. Copyright

**Definition:** Copyright protects original works of authorship including literary, dramatic, musical, artistic works, and software.

**What can be copyrighted in software?**
- Source code and object code
- User interfaces
- Documentation and manuals
- Screen displays

**Rights granted to copyright holder:**
- Right to reproduce (copy)
- Right to distribute copies
- Right to create derivative works (modifications)
- Right to publicly perform or display the work

**Duration:** Lifetime of author + 60 years (in India)

**Example:** Microsoft Word software code is copyrighted; you cannot copy and sell it.

---

### 2. Patent

**Definition:** A patent grants exclusive rights to an inventor for a new, useful, and non-obvious invention or process.

**For Software:** Patents can protect:
- New algorithms or processes
- Software-implemented inventions
- Unique methods of doing something in software

**Requirements for Patent:**
- Novelty (new and original)
- Inventive step (not obvious to experts)
- Industrial applicability (useful)

**Duration:** 20 years from filing date

**Example:** Amazon's "1-Click" ordering system was patented.

---

### Difference between Copyright and Patent:

| Aspect | Copyright | Patent |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Protects | Expression of idea | Underlying invention/process |
| Duration | 60+ years | 20 years |
| Registration | Automatic | Required |
| Cost | Low | High |

---

### 3. Trademark

**Definition:** A trademark is a recognizable sign, design, or expression that identifies products or services from a specific source.

**What can be trademarked:**
- Brand names (Google, Microsoft)
- Logos (Apple's apple logo)
- Slogans (Just Do It)
- Distinctive sounds or colors

**Duration:** 10 years (renewable indefinitely)

**Purpose:** Prevents confusion among consumers and protects brand reputation.

---

### 4. Industrial Design

**Definition:** Protects the visual design or ornamental aspects of a product.

**What it covers:**
- Shape
- Configuration
- Pattern
- Ornamentation

**Duration:** 10-15 years

**Example:** The unique look of an iPhone, or a distinctive software icon.

---

### 5. Geographical Indications (GI)

**Definition:** Indicates goods originating from a specific geographical territory.

**Example:** Darjeeling tea, Champagne from France

---

## 🔹 Confidential Information

**Definition:** Secret information that gives a business competitive advantage.

**Types of Confidential Information:**
- Trade secrets (Coca-Cola formula)
- Source code and algorithms
- Business strategies
- Customer lists

**How to protect:**
- NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement)
- Limited access to information
- Employee training
- Security measures

---

## 🔹 Copyright Infringement

**Definition:** Using copyrighted material without permission.

**Examples in Software:**
- Copying software without license (software piracy)
- Using proprietary code in your project
- Distributing cracked software

**Consequences:**
- Legal penalties (fines and imprisonment)
- Damages payable to copyright holder
- Injunction (court order to stop infringement)

---

## 🔹 Fair Use (Fair Dealing)

**Definition:** Limited use of copyrighted material without permission for specific purposes.

**Allowed purposes:**
- Education and research
- Criticism and review
- News reporting
- Parody

**Factors considered:**
1. Purpose of use (commercial or educational)
2. Nature of copyrighted work
3. Amount used
4. Effect on potential market value

---

## 🔹 Licensing vs Assignment

| Aspect | License | Assignment |
|--------|---------|------------|
| What is transferred | Right to use | Full ownership |
| Duration | Limited time period | Permanent |
| Control | Creator retains rights | Creator gives up rights |
| Revocation | Possible | Cannot revoke |

---

## 🔹 Moral Rights

**Definition:** Rights that protect the personal connection between creator and their work.

**Two main moral rights:**

1. **Right of Attribution (Paternity):** Author must be identified as creator
2. **Right of Integrity:** Author can prevent distortion or modification

**Key Points:**
- Moral rights remain with author even after selling copyright
- Cannot be transferred or sold

---

## 🔹 Passing Off

**Definition:** Misrepresenting goods/services as those of another party.

**Elements:**
1. Goodwill/Reputation
2. Misrepresentation
3. Damage

**Example:** Creating "MicroSoft" software with similar logo to "Microsoft"

---

## 🔹 Domain Names

**Definition:** Website address (example.com)

**IPR Connection:**
- Domain names can be trademarks
- Cybersquatting: Registering famous brand domains to sell

---

## 🔹 Key Case Laws

1. Apple v. Samsung (2012) - Design patent infringement
2. Oracle v. Google (2021) - Copyright for Java APIs
3. R.G. Anand v. Deluxe Films (1978) - Copyright principles

---

## 🔹 Conclusion of Unit I

IPR provides creators with legal protection and business value. Understanding IPR helps software engineers protect their work, avoid infringement, and respect others' intellectual property.

# 🟢 UNIT–II: Software Licenses

## 🔹 Introduction

**Definition:** A software license is a legal agreement that defines how software can be used, modified, and distributed.

**Why licenses are needed:**
- Without license, no one has right to use your software
- Sets clear rules for users
- Protects developer's rights
- Prevents legal disputes

---

## 🔹 Types of Software Licenses

### 1. Proprietary License

**Characteristics:**
- Source code is hidden (closed source)
- Requires payment (usually)
- Restrictions on copying, modifying, redistributing

**Examples:** Microsoft Windows, Adobe Photoshop, Oracle Database

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Company protects investment | Expensive |
| Quality support | User has no freedom |

---

### 2. Open Source License

**Definition:** Allows users to freely use, modify, and distribute software.

**Open Source Definition (OSI criteria):**
1. Free redistribution
2. Source code available
3. Allows modifications
4. No discrimination
5. No restrictions on other software

**Examples:** Linux, Firefox, Android (AOSP)

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Free | No warranty |
| Customizable | May have less support |
| Community support | |

---

## 🔹 Difference: Free Software vs Open Source

| Aspect | Free Software | Open Source |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Philosophy | Ethical freedom | Practical benefits |
| Focus | User freedom | Better code |
| Organization | FSF | OSI |

---

## 🔹 Important Open Source Licenses

### 1. MIT License

**Features:**
- Most permissive license
- Minimal restrictions
- Can be used in proprietary software

**Requirement:** Include original copyright notice

**Allowed:** Commercial use, modification, distribution, private use

**Example users:** React, Node.js, jQuery

---

### 2. BSD License

**Features:**
- Similar to MIT, very permissive
- 3-clause version: Cannot use author's name to promote products

**Example users:** Go programming language, Nginx

---

### 3. GNU GPL (General Public License)

**Features:**
- Strong copyleft license
- Modified software must also be open source
- Source code must be provided

**Key rule:** If you distribute GPL software, you must share your source code

**Example users:** Linux kernel, WordPress, MySQL

---

### 4. GNU LGPL (Lesser General Public License)

**Features:**
- Weak copyleft
- Allows linking with proprietary software

**Difference from GPL:**
- GPL: Your whole program must be GPL
- LGPL: Can use library in proprietary software

---

### 5. Apache License 2.0

**Features:**
- Permissive like MIT
- Includes patent protection grant
- Protects users from patent lawsuits

**Example users:** Android, Apache HTTP Server, Kubernetes

---

### 6. Mozilla Public License (MPL)

**Features:**
- File-based copyleft
- Modified files must be open source
- Can combine with proprietary files

---

## 🔹 License Comparison Table

| License | Commercial Use | Modify | Distribute | Share Source | Patent Grant |
|---------|---------------|--------|------------|--------------|--------------|
| MIT | Yes | Yes | Yes | No | No |
| BSD | Yes | Yes | Yes | No | No |
| Apache 2.0 | Yes | Yes | Yes | No | Yes |
| GPL | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | No |
| LGPL | Yes | Yes | Yes | Partially | No |
| Proprietary | Restricted | No | No | No | No |

---

## 🔹 Importance of Software Licenses

1. **Legal Safety** - Protects from lawsuits
2. **Clarity** - Defines what users can/cannot do
3. **Business Model** - Enables selling software
4. **Collaboration** - Open source enables sharing
5. **Compliance** - Ensures proper attribution

---

## 🔹 How to Choose a License

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Want to share source code? | → Open source |
| Want to sell licenses? | → Proprietary |
| Want modifications shared back? | → GPL |
| Want maximum users, no restrictions? | → MIT |

---

## 🔹 Conclusion of Unit II

Software licenses are essential legal tools that define proper software use. Engineers must understand licenses to comply with legal requirements and choose appropriate licenses for their own software.

# 🟢 UNIT–III: Software Contracts

## 🔹 Introduction

**Definition:** A software contract is a legally binding agreement between parties (client and developer/vendor) that defines terms for software development, delivery, and maintenance.

**Purpose:**
- Defines scope of work
- Specifies deliverables and timelines
- Sets payment terms
- Allocates risks and liabilities
- Provides legal protection

---

## 🔹 Essential Elements of a Valid Contract

| Element | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Offer** | One party makes a proposal | "I will develop software for ₹5 lakhs" |
| **Acceptance** | Other party agrees | Client signs the proposal |
| **Consideration** | Something of value exchanged | Money for software |
| **Intention** | Legal binding intent | Written agreement |
| **Capacity** | Legal capability | Adults of sound mind |
| **Lawful Object** | Legal purpose | Not for illegal activity |

---

## 🔹 Types of Software Contracts

### 1. Fixed Price Contract

**Definition:** Client pays a predetermined fixed price for defined deliverables.

**Risk Allocation:**
- Higher risk on developer (if project takes longer, developer loses money)
- Lower risk on client (knows exact cost)

**Best for:** Well-defined requirements, small to medium projects

**Example:** "Build an e-commerce website for ₹3 lakhs, deliver in 3 months"

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Client knows exact cost | Requirements changes difficult |
| Developer motivated to work efficiently | Developer may rush quality |

---

### 2. Time & Material Contract (T&M)

**Definition:** Client pays based on actual time spent and materials used.

**Risk Allocation:**
- Higher risk on client (final cost unknown)
- Lower risk on developer (paid for all work)

**Best for:** Unclear requirements, long-term projects, Agile development

**Example:** "₹2,000 per developer hour + software licenses"

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Flexible to change requirements | Total cost unknown |
| Client pays only for actual work | Client must monitor hours |

---

### 3. Service Contract / Maintenance Contract

**Definition:** Agreement for ongoing services like support, maintenance, updates.

**Common services:** Technical support, bug fixes, security updates, monitoring, training

**Service Level Agreement (SLA):**
- Response time (e.g., "Respond in 1 hour")
- Resolution time (e.g., "Fix in 24 hours")
- Uptime guarantee (e.g., "99.9% uptime")

---

## 🔹 Comparison: Fixed Price vs T&M

| Aspect | Fixed Price | Time & Material |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Cost certainty | High | Low |
| Flexibility | Low | High |
| Client risk | Low | High |
| Developer risk | High | Low |
| Best for | Clear requirements | Evolving requirements |

---

## 🔹 Liability in Software Contracts

**Definition:** Legal responsibility for damages caused by software failure.

**Types of Liability:**
1. **Contractual Liability** - Defined in contract
2. **Tort Liability (Negligence)** - Even if not in contract
3. **Product Liability** - Software as product

**Common Liability Clauses:**

| Clause | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Warranty | "Software free from defects for 90 days" |
| Disclaimer | "Software provided AS IS" |
| Limitation | Caps maximum liability |
| Indemnification | Developer pays if client is sued |

---

## 🔹 Custom Software Contracts

**Key considerations:**
- Who owns the code? (Assignment clause)
- Can developer reuse components?
- Acceptance testing process

**Custom vs COTS (Commercial Off-The-Shelf):**

| Aspect | Custom Software | COTS |
|--------|----------------|------|
| Fit | Exactly matches needs | Generic |
| Cost | Higher | Lower |
| Time | Longer | Immediate |
| Ownership | Client owns | Vendor owns |

---

## 🔹 Key Contract Clauses

1. **Scope of Work (SOW)** - Detailed deliverables
2. **Acceptance Testing** - Verification process
3. **Change Management** - How changes are handled
4. **Payment Terms** - Amount, schedule, method
5. **Confidentiality** - Protection of sensitive info
6. **Intellectual Property** - Who owns the code
7. **Termination** - How contract ends
8. **Dispute Resolution** - Mediation, arbitration, court
9. **Force Majeure** - Natural disasters, war

---

## 🔹 Conclusion of Unit III

Software contracts provide clarity and legal protection. Understanding different contract types helps engineers negotiate better terms and manage project risks.

# 🟢 UNIT–IV: Software Crime Prevention

## 🔹 Introduction to Cyber Crimes

**Definition:** Cyber crimes are illegal activities committed using computers, networks, or the internet.

**Why software crime is increasing:**
- More connected systems
- Valuable data online
- Anonymity available to criminals
- Lower risk of physical detection

---

## 🔹 Types of Cyber Crimes

### 1. Unauthorized Access (Hacking)

**Definition:** Accessing a computer system without permission.

**Common methods:**
- Password guessing/brute force
- Exploiting software vulnerabilities
- Social engineering
- Man-in-the-middle attacks

**Legal term:** Section 43(a) of IT Act, 2000 (India)

---

### 2. Fraud (Cyber Fraud)

**Definition:** Deceiving people to gain money or data.

**Common types:**
- Phishing (fake emails)
- Online shopping fraud
- Credit card fraud
- Investment scams

---

### 3. Data Theft

**Definition:** Stealing sensitive or confidential data.

**Targets:** Personal info, trade secrets, customer databases, login credentials

**Methods:** SQL injection, malware, insider threats

---

### 4. Data Alteration

**Definition:** Unauthorized modification or deletion of data.

**Impact:** Financial records changed, medical records altered, evidence tampered

---

### 5. Denial of Service (DoS) and DDoS

**Definition:** Making a system unavailable by overwhelming it with traffic.

**DoS:** Single source
**DDoS:** Multiple sources (botnet)

---

### 6. Other Cyber Crimes

| Crime | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Cyber Stalking | Repeated harassment online |
| Ransomware | Encrypting data, demanding payment |
| Identity Theft | Using someone else's identity |
| Software Piracy | Unauthorized copying |
| Cyber Terrorism | Political/ideological attacks |

---

## 🔹 Cyber Crime Prevention Techniques

### 1. Strong Passwords

**Best practices:**
- Minimum 12 characters
- Mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, symbols
- Different password for each service

**Bad:** `password123`
**Good:** `G7#kL9$mQ2&p`

---

### 2. Firewall

**Definition:** Monitors and controls network traffic.

**Types:**
- Network firewall (hardware)
- Host firewall (software)

**Example:** Windows Firewall blocks unknown incoming connections

---

### 3. Encryption

**Definition:** Converting data into unreadable format.

**Types:**
- Symmetric (AES) - same key
- Asymmetric (RSA) - public/private key

**Example:** HTTPS encrypts website traffic

---

### 4. Antivirus / Anti-malware

**Functions:**
- Detect known viruses
- Scan files and programs
- Remove or quarantine threats

**Example:** McAfee, Norton, Windows Defender

---

### 5. System Monitoring

**Monitor:** Login attempts, network traffic, file access, performance

**Tools:** IDS, IPS, SIEM

---

### 6. Access Control

**Principle of Least Privilege:** Minimum access needed

**Methods:** RBAC, Multi-factor authentication (MFA)

---

### 7. Backups

**3-2-1 Rule:** 3 copies, 2 media types, 1 offsite

**Purpose:** Recover from ransomware or corruption

---

### 8. Security Updates (Patching)

**Why important:** Vulnerabilities are discovered regularly

**Example:** Microsoft Patch Tuesday

---

### 9. Employee Training

**Topics:** Phishing recognition, password hygiene, incident reporting

---

## 🔹 Legal Framework in India (IT Act 2000)

| Section | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| 43 | Unauthorized access | Compensation up to ₹1 crore |
| 66 | Computer offenses | 3 years or ₹5 lakh fine |
| 66C | Identity theft | 3 years + fine |
| 66F | Cyber terrorism | Life imprisonment |
| 67 | Obscene content | 5 years + fine |

---

## 🔹 Incident Response Steps

1. **Preparation** - Have a plan ready
2. **Identification** - Detect incident
3. **Containment** - Stop the attack
4. **Eradication** - Remove malware
5. **Recovery** - Restore from backup
6. **Lessons Learned** - Improve security

---

## 🔹 Conclusion of Unit IV

Cyber security is essential for protecting systems and data. Software engineers must understand threats and implement prevention techniques.

# 🟢 UNIT–V: Data Protection and Privacy

## 🔹 Introduction

**Definition:** Data protection safeguards personal and sensitive data from unauthorized access, use, or destruction.

**Key concepts:**
- **Data privacy** - Who can access data
- **Data security** - Technical measures
- **Data protection** - Both combined

---

## 🔹 Importance of Data Protection

| Area | Why Important |
|------|---------------|
| User Trust | Users share data only when they trust |
| Legal Compliance | Laws require protection (GDPR, DPDP) |
| Business Reputation | Breaches damage brand image |
| Financial Impact | Fines, legal costs, compensation |
| Personal Harm | Prevent identity theft, fraud |

---

## 🔹 Types of Data

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Personal Data | Identifies individual | Name, address, phone |
| Sensitive Data | Extra protection needed | Health, biometrics, religion |
| Financial Data | Banking info | Credit card, bank account |
| Technical Data | System-related | IP address, device ID |
| Behavioral Data | User actions | Browsing history |

---

## 🔹 Impact of Internet on Data Protection

**Positive:**
- Easy data backup and recovery
- Cloud storage accessibility
- Automated security updates

**Negative:**
- Increased data sharing
- Data concentration (large databases)
- New attack vectors
- Cross-border data flow complexity
- Permanent data (hard to delete)
- Easy surveillance

---

## 🔹 Data Protection Techniques

### 1. Encryption

**Where to encrypt:**
- Data at rest (hard drives, databases)
- Data in transit (network, emails)
- Data in use (memory)

**Algorithms:** AES-256, RSA, TLS/SSL

**Example:** WhatsApp end-to-end encryption

---

### 2. Access Control

**Models:**
- DAC (Discretionary) - Owner decides
- MAC (Mandatory) - System enforces
- RBAC (Role-Based) - Access by job role

**Components:** Authentication, Authorization, Accounting

---

### 3. Backup and Recovery

**Strategies:**
- Full backup - Complete copy
- Incremental - Only changes
- Differential - Changes since last full

**Best practices:** Regular schedule, test restoration, encrypt backups

---

### 4. Authentication

| Factor | Examples |
|--------|----------|
| Something you know | Password, PIN |
| Something you have | Phone, smart card |
| Something you are | Fingerprint, face |

**MFA:** Using 2+ factors

---

### 5. Data Masking (Pseudonymization)

**Definition:** Replacing sensitive data with fake but realistic values.

**Example:** `John Smith` → `J*** S****`

**Anonymization:** Cannot be reversed
**Pseudonymization:** Can be reversed with additional data

---

### 6. Data Minimization

**Principle:** Collect and store only necessary data

**Benefits:** Less to protect, lower compliance burden

---

### 7. Secure Data Deletion

**Methods:**
- Overwriting (write random data)
- Degaussing (destroy magnetic fields)
- Physical destruction (shredding)
- Cryptographic erasure (delete encryption key)

---

## 🔹 Defamation

**Definition:** Damaging someone's reputation with false statements.

**Types:**

| Type | Medium | Description |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **Libel** | Written/permanent | Published text, images |
| **Slander** | Spoken/temporary | Spoken words |

**Cyber Defamation:** Online via social media, blogs, email, reviews

**Elements Required:**
1. False statement (truth is defense)
2. Published (shared with third party)
3. Identifies the person
4. Causes harm

**Defenses:**
- Truth
- Privilege (court, parliament)
- Fair Comment (opinion)
- Consent

---

## 🔹 Reputation Protection

**For Individuals:**
- Monitor online presence (Google yourself)
- Manage social media privacy
- Respond professionally to criticism
- Legal action if needed

**For Organizations:**
- Proactive reputation management
- Crisis communication plan
- Monitoring tools
- Legal remedies (defamation lawsuit)

---

## 🔹 Data Protection Laws

### India: DPDP Act 2023

**Key provisions:**
- Consent required for data collection
- Data Principal rights (access, correction, deletion)
- Data Fiduciary obligations
- Penalties up to ₹250 crore

### International: GDPR (Europe)

**Key features:**
- Right to be forgotten
- Data portability
- Breach notification (72 hours)
- Fines up to €20 million or 4% of global revenue

---

## 🔹 Privacy by Design (7 Principles)

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| 1. Proactive | Prevent issues before they occur |
| 2. Default | Most protective settings by default |
| 3. Embedded | Built into system, not added later |
| 4. Full functionality | No trade-offs |
| 5. End-to-end | Protect throughout lifecycle |
| 6. Transparency | Open and accountable |
| 7. User-centric | Respect user privacy |

---

## 🔹 Conclusion of Unit V

Data protection is critically important. Software engineers must implement technical measures while respecting user privacy rights.
